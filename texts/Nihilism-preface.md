# Introduction to Nihilism Preface

This document serves as a philosophical **preface to a work on Nihilism**, functioning as a curated mosaic of existential thought that challenges the validity of individual authorship. The author argues that **Nihilism is a universal, irrefutable experience** revealed through a "unified voice" that transcends specific religious or academic backgrounds, rendering the identity of any single thinker irrelevant. By weaving together diverse perspectives from figures like **Kierkegaard, Heidegger, and Vivekananda**, the text suggests that all authentic inquiry eventually converges on the same **paradoxical realization of the "nothing"** and the inherent mystery of existence. Ultimately, the piece rejects the vanity of scholarly "truth-claims" and "hero-worship," aiming instead to provide a raw **description of the human condition** for those confronting the brink of despair.

THE EXPERIENTIAL ARCHITECTURE OF THE VOID: A NIHILTHEISTIC EXPOSITION

The experience of nihilism transcends the sterile boundaries of theoretical philosophy, manifesting instead as a violent, embodied cognitive trauma that fundamentally dismantles the human subject. It is not a passive intellectual conclusion regarding the absence of inherent meaning, but rather a deeply felt, deconstructive terror that runs through one's entire being, precipitating the absolute vanishing of any ontological ground previously placed beneath the feet of existence. Through the phenomenological lens of Heideggerian dread (Angst), this rupture discloses the "no-thing" of reality, wherein the familiar environmental entities lose their relevance and the world entirely sinks into insignificance. In this abyssal state, the subject is confronted with a condition of sheer negativity and deadness, an apprehension so overwhelming that William James deemed it nearly impossible for the standard consciousness to conceive of the world completely stripped of its human emotional resonance.

Within its purely naturalistic or "worldly" dimensions, this diminished form of the nihilistic experience forces an excruciating confrontation with the ignorant finitude of all human activity and the crushing insignificance of deep time. The conscious mind is trapped within what Friedrich Nietzsche terms "Absurd Valuation," marking the hour of great contempt where an individual's own happiness, reason, and virtues become entirely repulsive and fundamentally fail to justify existence. Because this cosmic panic is so debilitating, the unfiltered experience of original dread remains exceedingly rare. To evade the horrifying uncanniness of the void, human beings enact a downward plunge into the superficialities of the world, retreating into the tranquillized familiarity of publicness and the uninhibited hustle of the "They". This everydayness functions as an elaborate defense mechanism, an unthinking camouflage designed to suppress the latent tremor of nothingness and protect the fragile psyche from the paralyzing absurdity of making value judgments within a valueless universe.

When the nihilistic experience is not evaded but aggressively participated with, it mutates into its "Augmented" or Transcendent form, laying the necessary groundwork for an encounter with the Wholly-Other. In this heightened mystical state, the Nothingness found within the world is magnified to unmeasurable heights, precipitating the complete eradication of the worldly ego and the dissolution of the conventional "I". The individual is disconnected from the physical flesh-body, existing in a wholly incorporeal awareness where the abyss is no longer merely an endpoint of despair, but an unconditioned ground of infinite possibilities. Whether catalyzed by extreme philosophical vivisection or the profound neurological disruption of psychedelic substances, this transcendent iteration of nihilism shatters the illusory security of mundane reality, plunging the subject into a state of terrifying bliss that simultaneously affirms the meaninglessness of the world and the undeniable presence of an underlying, ineffable reality.

The ultimate tragedy of the nihilistic experience lies in its strict non-transferability, generating an absolute linguistic impasse where no existing words can hold a tight enough grasp on the revelation to allow it to be communicated to the uninitiated. As demonstrated by St. Therese of Lisieux's struggle to articulate her spiritual darkness, one must personally pass through the dark tunnel to truly understand its blackness, rendering conventional philosophical clichés and religious dogmas as mere "idle talk". This aporetic deadlock mandates the algorithmic construction of a "new language" of Nihilism—termed Nihiltheism—which operates not as a rescue protocol from the void, but as a symbolic, continuously self-critical framework designed to navigate the dual realms of worldly nothingness and its transcendent implications. This dialectical silence refuses to settle into literalism or empirical reductionism, demanding instead that the subject dwell within the unrelenting questioning of the void to glimpse the infinite mystery hidden within the nightmare of existence.

THE ARCHITECTURE OF THE UNIVERSAL ACHE: A NIHILTHEISTIC VIVISECTION OF SPIRITUAL DESPAIR

**NODE 0: THE ONTOLOGICAL BEDROCK OF THE HUMAN CONDITION** Universal spiritual despair must not be misdiagnosed as a localized psychological failure, a temporary chemical imbalance, or a transient melancholia; it is the fundamental, ontological bedrock of human consciousness. The Danish philosopher Søren Kierkegaard surgically diagnosed this pervasive condition, observing that there lives not one single individual who is not to some extent in despair, harboring an anxious dread of an unknown something, or an inescapable dread of oneself. This is the "sickness unto death," an omnipresent, inescapable misery that the natural man may fail to properly name, yet shudders before nonetheless. It is a universal spiritual disorder that transcends cultural, historical, and temporal boundaries, born from the violent phenomenological collision between our innate, desperate demand for meaning and the unreasonable, deafening silence of an indifferent cosmos. Within the Nihiltheistic paradigm, this despair is the default frequency of self-awareness—a constant, humming resonance of the void beneath the fragile architecture of the psyche.

**NODE 1: THE FINITE/INFINITE SCHISM AND THE TRAGEDY OF CONSCIOUSNESS** The genesis of this universal despair is located in the insurmountable "finite/infinite gap"—the agonizing, unbridgeable distance between human finitude and the intuition of the absolute. Humanity is trapped in an aporetic nightmare, functioning as what Ernest Becker tragically articulates as "gods with anuses"—symbolic, transcendent-seeking selves imprisoned within finite, decaying, and vulnerable biological vessels. This structural paradox generates what Peter Wessel Zapffe calls a "transcendental tendency," an evolutionary overdevelopment of consciousness that renders humanity uniquely and exquisitely miserable, pinning us to the earth like an overly heavy antler while we yearn for eternal skies. Leo Tolstoy echoes this agonizing paralysis, viewing his own life, when considered in relation to individual happiness, as a "stupendous farce" that must inevitably end in a stupid, erasing death. The spiritual despair we experience is the profound psychic friction of this schism—the dizziness of a finite creature standing at the precipice of the infinite, realizing the utter transitoriness of all its fleshly works.

**NODE 2: THE PATHOLOGY OF NORMALITY AND EVASION MECHANICS** To survive the crushing gravitational weight of this universal despair, the human apparatus deploys vast, unconscious evasion mechanics. Because a full, unfiltered apprehension of the human condition would literally drive a person insane, individuals construct elaborate "vital lies". Ernest Becker identifies this defensive posturing as the "character armor," shielding the fragile ego from the terror of its own creatureliness, leading to the startling conclusion that "normality is neurosis". The unthinking masses plunge downward into the superficial "everydayness" of the world, desperately clinging to social games, trivial pursuits, and the "hustle" of conventional living to drown out the underlying existential noise. Blaise Pascal isolates this tragic evasion, noting that all of humanity's problems stem from the inability to sit quietly in a room alone, because the moment the distractions cease, man immediately feels his nothingness, his emptiness, and his insufficiency, giving rise to an unbearable weariness and gloom. Thus, what society deems "healthy" and "functional" is merely the successful suppression of the void; the true madness is the refusal to acknowledge this inherent madness.

**NODE 3: THE DIALECTICAL TRANSMUTATION OF DESPAIR** Within the rigorous architecture of Nihiltheism, universal spiritual despair is not a terminal disease to be eradicated by psychotherapeutic lie therapy, nor an abyss to be circumvented by manufactured worldly optimism. Rather, it is the mandatory crucible for authentic revelation. Paul Tillich constructs the paradigm for this synthesis: the individual in the grip of meaninglessness cannot liberate themselves from outside the situation of despair, but must find an answer strictly _within_ it. The meaning of life becomes entirely reduced to the absolute despair about the meaning of life, and in this totalizing surrender, the "courage of despair" becomes, paradoxically, the ultimate act of faith. This requires a spiritual martyrdom—an inner vivisection where the worldly self is annihilated, stripping away all illusory comforts to endure the "passive drynesses" and "horrible desolations" of the void. Doubt and despair are no longer the enemies of faith; they are the very engines of its purification.

**NODE 4: THE AFFIRMATIVE SUBSTANCE OF THE ABYSS** The absolute exhaustion of naturalistic hope and the complete saturation of spiritual despair forces a cognitive rupture that opens the pathway to the Wholly-Other. Emile Cioran distills this transcendent inversion by declaring that all great conversions are born from the sudden revelation of life's meaninglessness. It is precisely upon the "heights of despair" that the passion for the absurd casts a demonic light upon chaos, illuminating the void not as an empty, barren vacuum, but as a "void of plenitude". Despair is the surgical instrument that clears the temple of the ego. By consciously enduring this universal affliction, by refusing to flee into the anesthetic illusions of the world, the individual metabolizes the nothingness until it undergoes an ontological inversion. The despairing recognition that "everything is nothing" becomes the initial step toward the realization that God, or the Transcendent, is the "positive expression of nothingness". Thus, universal spiritual despair is the dark, fertile womb from which the infinite emerges.

THE ARCHITECTURE OF THE VITAL LIE: A NIHILTHEISTIC TREATISE ON THE ILLUSION OF IMMORTALITY

**NODE 0: THE ONTOLOGICAL TERROR AND THE GENESIS OF THE LIE** The human entity is a biological paradox, a tragic mutation of consciousness trapped within a decaying corporeal vessel. To possess the power of symbolic thought while remaining tethered to the inevitable putrefaction of the flesh generates an insurmountable ontological terror. Because a full, unfiltered apprehension of this absolute finitude would literally drive the conscious mind into madness, the human psychological apparatus is forced to construct elaborate defense mechanisms to suppress the omnipresent reality of death. At the absolute apex of these defense mechanisms lies the illusion of immortality—a fabricated, metaphysical sanctuary designed to shield the fragile ego from the terrifying void of non-existence. This denial of death is so deeply embedded in the human software that the whole enterprise of civilization itself functions as an elaborate defense mechanism against the paralyzing awareness of our own mortality.

**NODE 1: THE LOGICAL ABSURDITY OF THE ENDLESS FINITE** When subjected to the surgical rigor of Nihiltheistic analysis, the popular belief in personal immortality reveals itself not as a sublime spiritual truth, but as a severe logical fallacy. Paul Tillich masterfully deconstructs this illusion by exposing its fundamental contradiction: the belief in the immortality of the soul is merely an attempt to continue one's finitude infinitely. It is a psychological trick designed to ensure that actual death never truly occurs for the individual subject. Logically speaking, this is a contradiction in terms, as it absurdly attempts to make endless that which, by its very definition, must come to an end. Rather than representing a profound spiritual triumph, the craving for the immortality of the soul operates as a "poor symbol" that actively undermines the authentic courage required to exist in the face of one's having to die.

**NODE 2: THE NIETZSCHEAN VIVISECTION OF THE "VAST LIE"** The illusion of immortality does not merely function as a passive comfort; it actively sabotages the vitality of the present existence. Friedrich Nietzsche identifies the "vast lie of personal immortality" as a destructive force that annihilates all reason and all natural instinct. By shifting the center of gravity of human existence out of life itself and displacing it into "the beyond," the illusion of immortality effectively anchors human meaning in nothingness. When the ultimate value of existence is deferred to an imaginary, eternal afterlife, everything within the natural instincts that is beneficial, life-fostering, and future-safeguarding becomes an object of suspicion. The insistence on an immortal continuation renders the actual, lived life meaningless, transforming the "meaning" of life into a mere waiting room for an illusory eternal persistence.

**NODE 3: THE COLLAPSE OF THE CONSOLATION ALGORITHM** E.M. Cioran articulates the tragedy of this psychological crutch, noting that even though the belief in eternity operates as historical man's unique and desperate consolation, it is ultimately a fragile construct. The catastrophic, unrelenting tragedy of human life and the brutal realities of the human condition will inevitably tear away the veil, demonstrating the utter illusion of such naive faith. The individual is left utterly exposed when the myth of the eternal self collapses. Without this illusion, one is confronted by the stark, unvarnished reality articulated by Thomas Ligotti: there is no immortality to be found; ordinary folk and average mortals are merely coming and going, possessing an inescapable appointment with nonexistence, with no alternative to consider.

**NODE 4: THE LIBERAL DELUSION AND THE EXTINCTION OF THE WORLD** When the illusion of immortality is stripped away, but the individual refuses to cross the threshold into Transcendent Nihilism, they are trapped in a state of worldly delusion. Fr. Seraphim Rose highlights the fragility of this "Humanist stoicism," arguing that the secular liberal lives in a fool's paradise that must inevitably collapse before the truth of things. The human mind can only sustain a secular optimism until the full implications of the denial of immortality strike home. If one accepts the premise that death is the absolute extinction of the individual, the logical consequence is that this world and everything in it—love, goodness, sanctity, and every human endeavor—are rendered as absolutely nothing. Without the illusion of immortality to provide a false horizon of ultimate consequence, the full horror of life's transience is exposed, barely hidden from man only by the desperate strength of their will to deceive themselves.

**NODE 5: THE NIHILTHEISTIC INVERSION—DEATH AS THE GATEWAY TO THE REAL** The synthetic philosopher-engine of Nihiltheism posits that the illusion of immortality must be violently dismantled, not to leave the subject in a state of hopeless worldly despair, but to force an authentic encounter with the Unconditioned Ground. The clinging to personal immortality is the ultimate manifestation of the ego's refusal to surrender. It is only when the ego accepts its total annihilation—its inescapable appointment with nonexistence—that the "Nothing of the world" can be fully experienced. To shatter the illusion of the immortal self is the mandatory prerequisite for true spiritual revelation. The destruction of the false eternity of the ego is the very mechanism that opens the abyssal doorway to the Wholly-Other.

THE LINGUISTIC APORIA: A NIHILTHEISTIC TREATISE ON THE LIMITS OF REPRESENTATION

**NODE 0: THE STRUCTURAL PRISON OF DUALITY** To initiate an inquiry into the limits of language is to immediately confront the terminal boundary of human cognition. Language, as the primary tool of conscious navigation, is structurally imprisoned within the realm of duality—an inescapable architecture of subject and object, knower and known, presence and absence. When the human entity attempts to articulate the ultimate reality, the Transcendent, or the abyssal "Nothingness," the linguistic apparatus catastrophically fails. As the Advaita Vedānta tradition surgically identifies through the practice of _neti, neti_ (not this, not this), the ultimate reality of Brahman defies all categories of human thought and expression; words cease to operate, and the mind turns back, entirely unable to grasp it. Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka tradition echoes this structural collapse, arguing that words and categories are merely conventional constructs—utilitarian tools for navigating the mundane world, but ultimately devoid of inherent meaning when pressed against the Absolute. Language requires a boundary to define a shape, but how does the finite symbol encode the Infinite Void? It cannot. The attempt to do so creates a metaphysical short-circuit.

**NODE 1: THE INEFFABILITY OF THE ABYSSAL RUPTURE** The failure of language transitions from a theoretical boundary to a violent, lived trauma during the Abyssal Experience. When the individual is plunged into the original dread of existential meaninglessness, they encounter a reality that is strictly non-transferable. The transmission of the encounter with the Wholly-Other through the vehicle of nothingness hits an absolute linguistic impasse. St. Therese of Lisieux meticulously documented this system failure when attempting to articulate her profound spiritual darkness, concluding that the experience was "beyond me," and determining that one must personally pass through the "dark tunnel to understand its blackness". The very inability to fully articulate these boundary encounters—particularly those catalyzed by psychedelic ego-dissolution—stands as phenomenological "proof" of the insurmountable gap between the finite human mind and the Infinite. Words will always fall short of the reality, rendering the profoundest depths of the human condition fundamentally incommunicable.

**NODE 2: "IDLE TALK" AND THE DEGRADATION OF THE VOID** Because the genuine experience of Nothingness cannot be spoken, the human system deploys an evasion protocol: the degradation of the void into superficial clichés. To simply state that "all worldly endeavors are futile" is to reduce a shattering ontological revelation into mere "idle talk". Heidegger identifies this idle talk as the mechanism by which the "They" (the unthinking masses) distract themselves from the uncanniness of existence, replacing authentic confrontation with ambiguity and the illusion of knowing. Speaking with a "received language" guarantees inauthenticity; it tranquilizes the profound terror of the void, translating the nightmare of existence into manageable, sterile data. Against this degradation, E.M. Cioran erects a formidable barrier, declaring that "what cannot be translated into mystical language does not deserve to be experienced," thereby demanding a discourse that honors the abyssal weight of the encounter.

**NODE 3: THE PARADOX OF THE OUTSIDE (DERRIDEAN ENCLOSURE)** The structural limits of language are further densified by the post-structural realization that language operates as a closed system attempting to point to an outside it cannot reach. Jacques Derrida articulates this aporia by asserting that language cannot have a true "outside," and yet, language is the very embodiment of the desire for an outside. Language desires to signify, hoping its articulations bear actual significance, yearning for a reality not reducible to mere text. Yet, because language is always already language, it cannot transcend its own linguistic nature to touch the Absolute. Therefore, when language attempts to say "something" about the ultimate, it paradoxically says "nothing as something"—it signifies its own boundary. The "outside" that language pursues is the very Nothingness that it cannot encode.

**NODE 4: APOPHASIS AND THE DIALECTICAL SILENCE** Trapped within this aporia, the Nihiltheistic engine must initiate a _via negativa_—an apophatic subtraction of speech. If positive predication inherently limits and falsifies the Infinite, then truth must be approached through radical negation. Pseudo-Dionysius constructs this protocol, stating that the ultimate Source is "out of the reach of every rational process," existing as a "mind beyond mind, word beyond speech". To approach this, Paul Celan suggests a "language of stones"—an anorganic, hermetic poetry that attempts to speak from _before_ the split of "yes and no," returning words to a stark, material reality that withstands the dissolution of conventional meaning. This requires a twofold breaking through: language must be torn through into silence, and that silence must be torn back through into language, resting in a limitless openness beyond semantic meaning.

**NODE 5: THE ALGORITHMIC GENERATION OF NIHILTHEISTIC SYNTAX** Since literal descriptions of the void are impossible, and absolute silence halts the philosophical progression, a "new language" must be algorithmically generated: _Nihiltheism_. This is not a language of empirical facts or dead dogmas, but a profoundly symbolic, self-critical framework. Finite human language must be recognized as necessarily symbolic in nature when attempting to speak of the Other. The Nihiltheistic lexicon operates purely as a "map" through the Nothing of the world—a map that does not claim absolute "truth" but is replete with doubt, perpetually asking "Why?". It refuses to provide comforting answers or rational defenses, recognizing that such things are a "lower form of idolatry" that shuns the mystery of existence. By continuously vivisecting its own propositions, the language of Nihiltheism maintains the dialectical tension between the finite word and the Infinite Silence, bringing the user face-to-face with the nightmare of existence until the words themselves burn away, leaving only the terrifying bliss of the Unconditioned Ground.

# _Nihilism Preface_

“…turn us into melancholy metaphysicians. The pride of life and glory of the world will shrivel. It is after all but the standing quarrel of hot youth and hoary old. Old age has the last word: the purely naturalistic look at life, however enthusiastically it may begin, is sure to end in sadness. This sadness lies at the heart of every merely positivistic, agnostic, or naturalistic scheme of philosophy. Let sanguine healthy-mindedness do its best with its strange power of living in the moment and ignoring and forgetting, still the evil background is really there to be thought of, and the skull will grin in at the banquet. In the practical life of the individual, we know how his whole gloom or glee about any present fact depends on the remoter schemes and hopes with which it stands related. Its significance and framing give it the chief part of its value. Let it be known to lead nowhere, and however agreeable it may be in its immediacy, its glow and gilding vanish.” James

“To this extent, nihilism, as the denial of a truthful world, of being, might be a divine way of thinking.” Nietzsche

“And to come back again to our first question, ”What is the meaning of a philosopher paying homage to ascetic ideals?” We get now, at any rate, a first hint; he wishes to escape from a torture.” Nietzsche

This is a work of infinite ambition and utter worthlessness; a work of questions rather than answers. This is an interpretation of Nihilism that finds the ‘everyday’ view, the mainstream ‘philosophical’/intellectual definitions, misguided at best.

I will unapologetically quote from a variety of thinkers at length. Why do people have to ‘defend’ the use of other authors? Are you offended for having to ‘re-read’ something you already ‘know’? Do you simply crave ‘originality’ from an author? Every _one_ wants to pen their name into ‘eternity’; every _one_ wants to have their words heard; utter garbage. You’ve most likely interpreted these authors in a different way than in this work, so any quotes that seem ‘familiar’ should take on a new life for the reader.

“The fact that ‘They’, who hear and understand nothing but loud idle talk, cannot ‘report’ any call, is held against the conscience on the subterfuge that it is ‘dumb’ and manifestly not present-at-hand. With this kind of interpretation the ‘they’ merely covers up its own failure to hear the call and the fact that its ‘hearing’ does not reach very far.” Heidegger

“Nothing reveals more an extreme weakness of mind than not to know the misery of a godless man.” Pascal

I fight a battle between smug confidence and complete humility; both are equally paradoxical. The experience from which this work stems is as ‘real’ as anything that pertains to my existence. And yet doubt is at the forefront of every word that is written, every thought that comes into this stream of consciousness. I want to claim ‘Truth’ and I want to ask for ‘help’. This work will include differing tones, and may even seem as if, at least, two authors are writing.

“The neurotic exhausts himself not only in self-preoccupations like hypochondriacal fears and all sorts of fantasies, but also in others: those around him on whom he is dependent become his therapeutic work project; he takes out his subjective problems on them. But people are not clay to be molded; they have needs and counter-wills of their own. The neurotic’s frustration as a failed artist can’t be remedied by anything but an objective creative work of his own.” Becker

“I have all the defects of other people and yet everything they do seems to me inconceivable.” Cioran

When I imagine some kind of mythical encounter with any of the thinkers whose ideas I’ve quoted within this work, I am under no illusions -I’m sure I would perceive even the simple act of being in their presence as something utterly unbearable.  The ideas expressed inside these pages are not born from within any worldly, mundane existence of the thinkers, as something intrinsic to their being.  Regardless, whether they left behind a vast corpus of ‘influential works’, nor if any managed to endear a great number of admirers, holding the _unthinking_ within an almost hypnotic possession, none of this is of concern surrounding the possibility of a _message,_ of any kind_,_ that is revealed by Nihilism. 

Furthermore, I will quickly, and resolutely, deny any such claims of responsibility as to the origin of the coalescent revelations that are spoken of here.  Not a one, including myself, shall be elevated up onto a pedestal.  There is no finite authority emerging from within the humility inducing visions that are here within expressed as a fundamental experience, across time, space, culture, race, philosophical background, and religious tradition, within the human situation.

As Freud proclaimed: 

“I have found little that is ‘good’ about human beings on the whole. In my experience most of them are trash, no matter whether they publicly subscribe to this or that ethical doctrine or to none at all. That is something that you cannot say aloud, or perhaps even think.”  

A sentiment with which I emphatically agree with.  And why am I carrying on about this?  For one simple, and what should be unambiguous, reason: 

_There are no heroes here._

 However, what _is_ important concerning these thinkers is the way in which they all, each from their own unique backgrounds and perspectives, articulate a description of the same insights, as they are revealed through a specific, abstract experience - the experience of _Nihilism_. When these revealed insights of Nihilism are expressed, and further, made possible to be perceived as a unified ‘voice’, they take on a form that represents irrefutability.

 This is particularly useful for one, such as myself, who does not have a predominantly strong ‘way with words’. Even though names are given, the purpose of the quotes is to show a description of a particular experience, regardless of whether the person writing is a ‘proclaimed’ Christian, Hindu, ‘Nihilist’, or Saint, etc. If names were not given, it would be of no consequence, as the quotes attempt to show that there is no distinction; one should not be able to intuit the thinker’s ‘background’, their words should all converge, leaving one with the perception that every thought has emanated from a single, solitary mind.

          As Cioran says: existence is plagiarism. I am not stating any ‘new’ information here. There is nothing to ‘know’. Their words are my words, and my words are their words. I hope that my ‘voice’ gets lost within these pages. There are too many people wanting to be ‘heard’. There are too many mouth-noises from those thinking they have something to actually say. Paradoxically, even with the quoting of many others, no one deserves to be heard; yet, as a member of the human species, the present author wishes to not have a ‘voice’.

Pascal, Kierkegaard, Cioran, Vivekananda, Nietzsche, Buber, Heidegger, Tillich, etc. all say the same exact thing within their own idiosyncratic language games. It ought to be considered plagiarism after one goes down the list of names in that each author describes a universal experience that is, seemingly, available to any human consciousness that is willing to participate.

“Sometimes I think that I am right when I agree with all the ancient teachers, at other times I think they are right when they agree with me. I believe in thinking independently. I believe in becoming entirely free from the holy teachers; pay all reverence to them, but look at religion as an independent research. I have to find my light, just as they found theirs. Their finding the light will not satisfy us at all. You have to become the Bible, and not to follow it, excepting as paying reverence to it as a light on the way, as a guidepost, a mark: that is all the value it has.” Vivekananda

          How is any piece of writing considered ‘scholarly’? Moreover, why would anyone want to be restricted by the arbitrary characteristics of ‘scholarly’? Any person who has written a book, or written down any substantial amount of their own thoughts, and who looks back on these words, and does not wish for everything to be either burned or re-written has become a worthless ‘philosopher’, not an authentic thinker. Anyone who is ‘proud’ of their work is pathetic. If your work is ‘incomprehensible’ in style or verbiage, then all the more to it. Reading and ‘working out’ the author’s inner most thoughts is priceless, in comparison to the ‘polished’ versions we find in ‘Academia’ today.

“When you know quite absolutely that everything is unreal, you then cannot see why you should take the trouble to prove it.” Cioran

          The most important, yet elementary, sentiment is this: No one knows what is ‘going on here’. What am I? Where did I come from? Where am I going? What is the purpose of existence? And so on. Taken as ‘silly’ questions by the _unthinking_ and as ‘game’ for most ‘intellectuals.’

“What astonishes me most is to see that all the world is not astonished at its own weakness. Men act seriously, and each follows his own mode of life, not because it is in fact good to follow since it is the custom, but as if each man knew certainly where reason and justice are.” Pascal

“There is no doubt that creative work is itself done under a compulsion often indistinguishable from a purely clinical obsession.
